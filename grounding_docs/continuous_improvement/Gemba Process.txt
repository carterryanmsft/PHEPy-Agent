# **1. What a Gemba Walk Is (Foundational Understanding)**

A Gemba Walk is the deliberate act of **going to the real place where work happens** (physically or virtually) to observe how a process actually operates. The purpose is to gain deep, firsthand understanding of how work is performed and how value flows to the customer.

Core principles:

*   Focus on **direct observation**, not assumptions or documentation alone.
*   Seek to understand the **true workflow**, including variation, delays, and workarounds.
*   Engage directly with the individuals doing the work to learn from their reality.
*   Capture **real data**, customer‑relevant outcomes, and constraints.

It is both a learning activity and a structured diagnostic tool within continuous improvement.

***

# **2. Why Gemba Walks Matter (Purpose & Value)**

The documents align on three primary reasons:

### **2.1 Gain deep insight into the real workflow**

Observing work in its natural environment exposes:

*   hidden bottlenecks
*   sources of variation
*   informal practices or workarounds
*   misalignments between expected and actual processes

### **2.2 Identify improvement opportunities**

Data gathered during the walk highlights:

*   delays
*   unnecessary steps
*   rework
*   quality issues
*   resource gaps
*   tool or system limitations

### **2.3 Align leadership and stakeholders**

Bringing leaders into the real environment builds:

*   shared understanding
*   empathy for constraints employees face
*   clear justification for change
*   cross‑team alignment on priorities

Ultimately, Gemba Walks ensure improvement efforts are grounded in **reality** rather than assumptions or historical reports.

***

# **3. When to Use a Gemba Walk**

Gemba Walks are most valuable when:

*   **Evaluating a process end‑to‑end**
*   **Investigating problems** or defects
*   **Exploring pain points** experienced by customers or employees

They are a core tool within Continuous Improvement and support early stages of diagnostic frameworks (e.g., DIVE).

***

The two documents describe consistent roles:

### **4.1 Process Owner**

*   Provides context
*   Facilitates the walk

*   Helps identify improvement areas

### **4.2 Process Executors** (the people who do the work)

### **4.3 Leaders / Observers**

*   Surface improvement opportunities

The emphasis is on **collaboration**, curiosity, and respect.

# **5. End‑to‑End Gemba Walk Execution Flow**

The two documents collectively lay out a structured sequence for conducting a Gemba Walk. The integrated flow is as follows:

***

## **Step 1 — Select a Representative Example**

Identify a real‑life transaction, case, workflow, or customer experience that captures typical process behavior.  
This becomes the anchor for the observation.

Best practices:

*   Pick an example illustrating the entire journey end‑to‑end.
*   If live observation is not possible, use recorded or previously captured events, ensuring they are uninterrupted and representative.

***

## **Step 2 — Experience the Process (Walk the Flow Backwards)**

Start from the **customer‑received output**, then walk backward step by step through the process.

This helps you:

*   Understand how value is created
*   Reveal upstream dependencies
*   See where delays or rework originate

This is foundational because walking backward exposes the true causal chain behind outcomes.

***

## **Step 3 — Document Data and Feedback**

Data collection during the walk includes:

*   **Process time (PT)** — active work time
*   **Lead time (LT)** — total time including delays, waits, transfers
*   **Resources** involved (people, systems, tools)
*   **Inputs** required for each step
*   **Outputs** produced
*   **Owners** of each step
*   **Defect rates**, variability, and rework
*   **Customer/employee feedback** from the real experience

One document provides a detailed question set for each category:

### Inputs

*   What inputs are required?
*   Where do inputs come from?
*   Are they consistent or variable?
*   How often are they insufficient?

### Activities

*   What work is performed?
*   What tools/systems are used?
*   What drives variation?

### Outputs

*   What is produced?
*   Who receives it?
*   How is it handed off?
*   How stable is the output?

### Customer experience

*   Does the customer interact with this step?
*   What is their experience like?

### Reflections

*   What is the hardest part?
*   What often needs rework?
*   How much time is active vs. waiting?

***

## **Step 4 — Create a Value Stream Map (VSM)**

The VSM is both a documentation method and an organizing framework for the walk.

VSM captures:

1.  Process steps
2.  Process and lead times
3.  Inputs/outputs
4.  People and systems involved
5.  Data related to waste, defects, and variation

Value Stream Mapping can optionally be created **before** the walk to guide discussion, but should be validated during live observation.

***

## **Step 5 — Identify Improvement Opportunities**

After consolidating the data:

*   Discuss findings with the team
*   Prioritize the issues
*   Align on next steps
*   Identify where automation, AI, better data, or streamlined processes could improve outcomes

The documents emphasize:

*   Collaborative prioritization
*   Alignment with customer value
*   Ensuring next steps are actionable

***

# **6. Preparation Guidance (Pre‑Walk Considerations)**

The training deck adds a preparatory step asking leaders to reflect on key questions before observing:

*   Is the process meeting its targets?
*   Is performance improving or worsening?
*   What are customers telling us?
*   Where might automation/AI have significant impact?
*   Where do we lack data today that would improve understanding?

These prompts help shape focus areas and prepare observers to engage meaningfully.

***

# **7. Wrap‑Up (Post‑Walk Activities)**

After the walk and analysis:

*   Collect feedback from participants
*   Reassess where the initiative sits within the DIVE framework
*   Direct teams toward additional resources (Quick Guide, VSM templates, training deck)
*   Establish accountability for next steps

The Walk is not merely an isolated observation — it is part of a broader improvement cycle.

***

# **8. Integrated End‑to‑End Flow (Single Narrative Summary)**

If we merge all content into a single smooth flow, a Gemba Walk looks like this:

1.  **Prepare** by understanding business performance, customer feedback, and improvement hypotheses.
2.  **Select** a representative real‑life example illustrating the customer’s end‑to‑end experience.
3.  **Go to the work**, physically or virtually, and start from the customer outcome.
4.  **Walk backward** step by step, observing how work is actually performed.
5.  **Engage the people doing the work** to understand constraints, inputs, outputs, tools, and variability.
6.  **Document everything** — times, resources, delays, handoffs, challenges, and reflections.
7.  **Build or validate a Value Stream Map** summarizing the true flow of work.
8.  **Identify root issues, waste, and improvement opportunities** grounded in real observation.
9.  **Align leaders and teams** on what needs to change and why.
10. **Prioritize next steps** and plug results into the broader continuous improvement or DIVE framework.
